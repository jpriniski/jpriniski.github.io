"We Must Train Students to Season and Trim"

I. Hook: The Wrong Problem
AI skeptics in education are asking "how do we stop students from using AI to write?" This is the wrong question. The more honest question is: what were we training them to write for in the first place?

II. The Factory Before the Robot: How Standardized Testing Already Killed Student Voice
The five-paragraph essay was not pedagogy, it was a production spec. Walk through the canonical structure students were drilled on: thesis, three body paragraphs, conclusion, repeat. Then zoom out to the SAT/ACT writing pipeline.

Key points:
- Standardized grading systems can only assess surface features like passive voice and sentence length, flagging stylistic choices found in the best published writing, while completely missing the quality of a writer's ideas and the appropriateness of their choices. (ASCD)
- When the focus is solely on test performance, there is no institutional interest in what a student actually thinks or believes. Testing alignment micromanages what students learn and matches it to the test, but does little to engage them.
- There is little real connection between quality instruction and standardized test performance. A student who learned to write thoughtful, original essays about things they genuinely care about, in language that draws on their own cultural experience, would be no better equipped to ace the ACT's arbitrary word-choice questions.

The students who survived this system with their voice intact were often those with the social capital to take risks: private school kids, legacies, the future Gaitlin NYU crowd whose AI skepticism now gets op-ed real estate. For everyone else, idiosyncrasy was a liability.

III. What AI Actually Changes (If We Let It)
AI does not kill voice. The factory already did that. What AI can do, deployed correctly, is free educators from being grammar referees so they can actually coach perspective.

- AI-mediated tools influence student engagement through personalization alignment, adapting instructional pace and content to individual learner profiles, and through structured feedback that enhances metacognitive awareness.
- Students who received AI support in writing produced stronger arguments and engaged in deeper revision than those relying solely on peer feedback, particularly when using structured prompts focused on genre-specific goals.

But AI is not a ghostwriter. The human must still seed the prompt with a real perspective, verify the links actually work, apply common sense, and integrate background knowledge. The machine formats; the person thinks.

IV. Season and Trim: A New Framework for Writing with AI
Replace the five-paragraph essay with a new set of steps. Each step names what the human is doing and where their judgment is irreplaceable.

Proposed steps (to be developed):
1. Stake -- Identify and commit to a distinct perspective before touching the AI. What do you actually believe about this?
2. Seed -- Feed the AI your position, your examples, your voice markers. You are the source material.
3. Draft -- Let AI generate a structural scaffold or rough draft based on your seeds.
4. Verify -- Check every claim, every link, every statistic. Does this actually say what it claims? Does this link work?
5. Season -- Rewrite flat or generic passages in your own register. Add the specificity only you carry.
6. Trim -- Cut what doesn't serve the argument. AI over-produces; the writer decides what stays.
7. Own it -- Read the final piece aloud. If a sentence doesn't sound like you, rewrite it. Your name is on this.

V. The Class Critique: Who Benefits From Keeping the Old System
The loudest voices warning students away from AI are often those who never needed the five-paragraph essay to get through the door. Name the dynamic directly. The old gatekeeping system did not produce better writers; it produced better test-takers from families who could afford the prep. AI threatens that sorting mechanism.

VI. Close: Flip the Burden of Proof
The question is not whether AI belongs in writing education. The question is why we are defending a system that was already failing students before AI arrived. Challenge the skeptics: if your concern is authentic student voice, where was that concern during the era of drill-and-kill SAT prep?
